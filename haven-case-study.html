Haven: Perinatal Mental Health Platform

Where Support Finds You

A human-centered digital platform providing anonymous mental health support, community, and education for new and expecting parents in low-resource communities.

Role UX Researcher, Product Designer
Team 4 members (Phoebe Bennett, Mohit Johar, Amy Li)
Timeline 12 weeks
Tools Figma, Miro, Google Forms

The Challenge

Perinatal mental health disorders affect 1 in 5 new parents, but parents in low-resource communities face unique barriers to accessing support:

  • Limited access to mental health professionals
  • Social stigma around mental health in their communities
  • Fragmented information across multiple disconnected sources
  • Fear of judgment when seeking help
  • Lack of peer support networks

Current solutions—from sparse community resources to expensive therapy apps—fail to address the specific needs of parents who rely on community centers and need anonymous, accessible, evidence-based support.

How might we create a supportive digital experience that meets parents where they are, without adding burden or stigma?

Understanding Our Users

Research Methods

To understand the needs of our target users, we conducted:

  • 15+ in-depth interviews with new and expecting parents
  • Survey with 50+ respondents from community centers
  • Stakeholder interviews with social workers and care providers
  • Competitive analysis of existing perinatal mental health resources
  • Literature review of perinatal mental health research

[Research process photos or affinity mapping visualization]

Key Insights

1. Anonymity is Essential

Parents expressed fear of judgment and concerns about privacy. Many were comfortable seeking information but hesitant to share personal struggles under their real names.

"I don't want people in my community knowing I'm struggling. What if they think I'm a bad parent?" — Interview participant, first-time mother

2. Information Overload Creates Anxiety

Parents found existing resources overwhelming and contradictory. They wanted curated, evidence-based information tailored to their specific stage (pre-natal vs. post-natal).

3. Community Connection Reduces Isolation

Parents valued peer support over professional advice for emotional validation. Connecting with others in similar situations helped reduce feelings of isolation.

4. Fathers Need Dedicated Spaces

Male caregivers felt excluded from existing resources designed primarily for mothers. They wanted spaces specifically for fathers to discuss their unique challenges.

Defining the Problem Space

[Value Proposition Canvas visualization]

User Wants

  • Safety and privacy
  • Quality, evidence-based information
  • Education on parenting and mental health
  • To feel heard and validated
  • Sense of community and belonging

User Fears

  • Losing their child or parenting incorrectly
  • Social stigma and judgment
  • Isolation and losing their identity
  • Not being a "good enough" parent

Design Requirements

Based on our research, we established that Haven must:

Must Have
  • Anonymous access (no account required)
  • Privacy-first architecture
  • Evidence-based educational content
  • Community forums with safety moderation
  • Crisis support resources
  • Mobile-responsive design
Should Have
  • Personalized content recommendations
  • Real-time chat functionality
  • Event calendar for community activities
  • Separate spaces for fathers/mothers/caregivers

Design Decisions

Color Psychology

We chose soft, calming colors (pink, teal) associated with comfort and safety. These colors test well with our target demographic and create a welcoming, non-clinical feel.

[Color palette with rationale]

Typography & Accessibility

We selected friendly, readable fonts with strong accessibility standards. All text meets WCAG AAA color contrast ratios throughout the platform.

Illustration Style

Custom watercolor illustrations (flamingos, nature themes) create a gentle, approachable aesthetic that differs from clinical mental health resources. This reduces stigma and makes the platform feel more like a community space.

The Solution

Haven is an anonymous, mobile-first platform that meets parents where they are—offering evidence-based education, peer support, and crisis resources without judgment or barriers.

[Final UI mockups - Home screen and key features]

Key Features

1. Anonymous Guest Access

Users can explore education and community features without creating an account. Only usernames required for posting/chatting, ensuring privacy and lowering barriers to entry.

Why it matters: Removes fear of stigma and allows users to lurk before engaging.

[Guest user flow screenshot]

2. Personalized Recommendations

Smart algorithm tailors content based on:

  • Stage (pre-natal vs. post-natal)
  • Role (mother/father/caregiver)
  • Age (young parent option)

Why it matters: Reduces information overload and provides relevant, timely support.

[Personalization screen]

3. Safe Community Spaces

Discussion forums moderated by ML algorithm that flags inappropriate language. Separate spaces for fathers, mothers, young parents, and topic-specific groups.

Technical consideration: Algorithm improves over time with feedback from care providers. Users can be removed and IP-blocked if necessary.

[Community forum screens]

4. Live Chat "Coffee Rooms"

Real-time chat limited to 10 users per room. Users can create private coffee rooms (minimum 3 users). Messages deleted after 3 weeks for privacy.

Why it matters: Provides immediate peer support while maintaining privacy safeguards.

[Chat interface screenshot]

5. Crisis Support Hub

One-tap access to crisis resources, including:

  • 24/7 helpline numbers
  • Evidence-based self-assessment tools
  • Immediate coping strategies
  • Local emergency services

Why it matters: Ensures users in crisis can get help quickly without navigation friction.

What I Learned

About Design Process:

  1. Anonymity ≠ No accountability
    We initially worried anonymous access would lead to toxic behavior. ML moderation + community guidelines proved effective. The key was designing systems that maintain safety without sacrificing privacy.
  2. Inclusion requires intentional design
    Our research with fathers revealed major gaps in existing resources. Creating dedicated spaces (rather than generic "parents") significantly improved engagement and satisfaction.
  3. Progressive disclosure reduces anxiety
    We learned to reveal features gradually rather than overwhelming users upfront. The onboarding flow asks questions one at a time, creating a calmer experience.

About Working in Teams:

  • Regular check-ins prevented misalignment
  • User testing early and often saved time
  • Dividing work by feature (not discipline) improved ownership

Reflection

This project taught me that the best solutions emerge when you deeply understand not just what users want, but what they fear. By prioritizing privacy, reducing stigma, and creating genuinely inclusive spaces, we designed a platform that addresses the root causes of why parents avoid seeking help.

The intersection of digital product design and mental health support requires careful ethical consideration. Every design decision—from anonymous access to message deletion policies—needed to balance user safety, privacy, and effectiveness. This project reinforced my commitment to responsible, human-centered design.
