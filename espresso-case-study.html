Minimalist Hand-Press Espresso Machine

Engineering Function and Form Through Iterative Prototyping

A portable, hand-operated espresso machine optimized for weight, size, and manufacturing simplicity through CAD, FEA simulation, and rapid prototyping.

Role Product Designer, Mechanical Engineer
Team 3 members (Yuhang Zheng, Alexis Macagno)
Timeline 8 weeks
Tools Fusion 360, Prusa 3D Printer, FEA

The Challenge

Espresso lovers face a dilemma: quality espresso machines are either:

  • Large and heavy (commercial machines: 30+ lbs)
  • Expensive ($500+)
  • Complex to operate and maintain
  • Not portable for travel or small spaces

Existing portable solutions compromise on quality or require excessive manual force, making them impractical for daily use.

Project Goals

Design and prototype a hand-press espresso machine that:

  1. Delivers proper espresso (9 bars of pressure, 40-55ml yield)
  2. Minimizes weight and footprint (40cm x 40cm max)
  3. Requires ≤30 lbs of force to operate
  4. Can be assembled/disassembled without tools
  5. Uses 3D printing for rapid prototyping and customization

Design Constraints

Technical Requirements:

  • Consistent 9 bar pressure delivery
  • Standard 40mm portafilter compatibility
  • Class 2 lever mechanism (mechanical advantage)
  • Maximum 30 lb force on handle

Manufacturing Constraints:

  • 3D printable components (Prusa)
  • Minimal machined parts (cost/complexity)
  • Assembly without specialized tools
  • Material strength vs. weight tradeoff

Concept Development

Mechanical Analysis

We needed to achieve 9 bars of pressure with ≤30 lbs of force. This required understanding lever mechanics:

[Lever class diagram and force calculations]

Class 2 Lever Calculation:

  • Effort arm (handle): 400mm
  • Resistance arm (piston): 48mm
  • Mechanical advantage: 8.3:1
  • Result: 30 lbs input = 250 lbs output = 9 bars

This informed our geometry: the backbone height and handle length were mathematically optimized, not arbitrary aesthetic choices.

Initial CAD Design

[Early Fusion 360 models]

Our first design prioritized function:

  • Vertical backbone structure from base to piston
  • Horizontal handle connected via pivot pin
  • Sliding portafilter mount
  • Hook-and-cavity connection (no screws)

Design Philosophy: Modularity - Every component designed to assemble/disassemble easily, clean thoroughly, and replace individually.

Iterative Prototyping

Test Print Strategy

Rather than printing the full assembly, we tested critical connection points in isolation. This saved time and material while revealing design flaws early.

[Test prints - bearing and base-backbone joint]

Test 1: Base-Backbone Joint

Problem: Initial hook design was too narrow and fragile

Learning: Need to account for layer-line orientation in 3D printing

Solution: Widened hook, added support geometry

Test 2: Handle-Bearing Connection

Problem: Tolerance too tight, bearing wouldn't fit

Learning: Account for 0.2mm offset in FDM printing

Solution: Adjusted CAD dimensions, perfect fit achieved

Finite Element Analysis & Optimization

Testing Material Choices

We needed materials that:

  • Provide sufficient strength (safety factor >2)
  • Minimize weight and cost
  • Are food-safe and durable

[Von Mises stress visualizations for 4 materials]

Material Comparison

Material Yield (MPa) Tensile (MPa) Weight (g) Result
Nylon12 46 50 244 ✓ Chosen
Aluminum 6061 275 310 650 Too heavy
PAEK 100 210 318 Overkill
Polycarbonate 62 69 289 Acceptable

Why Nylon12 Won:

  • Lowest weight (244g for backbone)
  • Adequate strength with safety factor of 2.3x
  • Easily sourced and food-safe
  • Good printability

Critical Load Points

[Force diagram showing constraints]

The pivot point where the handle connects experiences the highest stress. Our FEA revealed:

  • Maximum stress: 19.8 MPa
  • Safety factor: 2.32 (well above minimum 2.0)
  • Maximum deformation: 0.8mm (acceptable)

Design Improvement

First FEA showed weakness at 90° angle where handle meets backbone. Solution: Rounded the transition, reducing stress concentration by 40%. Second FEA confirmed safe stress levels.

[Before/After FEA comparison showing stress reduction]

Shape Optimization

Used Fusion 360's generative design to explore weight reduction:

  • Input: Maintain stiffness, reduce mass 20-40%
  • Result: Organic lattice structure reduced weight by 32%
  • Decision: Kept simplified design for easier 3D printing

[Shape optimization process visualization]

Component Breakdown

1. Base (ABS)

  • Low center of gravity for stability
  • Cavity for backbone hook connection
  • Recessed area for portafilter drip collection

2. Backbone (Polycarbonate)

  • Optimized for strength-to-weight ratio
  • Integrated hook connection mechanism
  • Flat surface for portafilter slide-lock

3. Handle (Polycarbonate)

  • Ergonomic curve for comfortable grip
  • 400mm lever arm for mechanical advantage
  • Single-pin pivot connection

4. Portafilter (Machined Aluminum)

  • Standard 40mm basket (universal compatibility)
  • CNC machined for precision fit
  • Rubber gasket seal for leak prevention

5. Piston Assembly (Stainless Steel + Rubber)

  • Food-safe stainless steel construction
  • Silicone gasket for pressure seal
  • 48mm diameter (optimized for force distribution)

Manufacturing Plan

3D Printed Parts:

  • Print time: 12 hours total
  • Material cost: ~$15
  • Support structures: Minimal
  • Post-processing: Light sanding on mating surfaces

Machined Parts:

  • Portafilter: CNC aluminum (1 hour machine time)
  • Pin: Standard 8mm steel rod (off-the-shelf)

Assembly Process:

  1. Insert pin through backbone and handle bearing
  2. Hook backbone into base cavity
  3. Slide portafilter into lock position
  4. Insert piston assembly

Total time: <2 minutes, no tools needed

What I Learned

Design for Manufacturing (DFM):

The gap between "looks good in CAD" and "actually works when printed" taught me to respect manufacturing constraints early. Testing isolated components saved us from printing (and discarding) multiple full assemblies.

Material selection is a system decision:

We couldn't just pick "the strongest material." Weight, cost, printability, food-safety, and strength all had to be balanced. FEA helped make this decision data-driven rather than guesswork.

Iteration is non-linear:

We went back and forth between CAD and printer multiple times. Each test revealed new considerations (tolerances, orientation, support needs). Embracing this messy process led to a better result.

Engineering + Aesthetics:

The final design is minimal not just for style, but because every element serves a function. The curved handle isn't decoration—it's ergonomics. The open frame isn't minimalism—it's weight reduction. When form follows function, beauty emerges naturally.

Reflection

This project reinforced that good product design sits at the intersection of user needs, technical feasibility, and manufacturing reality. Every curve, dimension, and material choice was justified by constraint or requirement—nothing was arbitrary.

Working in CAD, FEA, and physical prototyping taught me to think in systems rather than individual components. The portafilter affects the backbone. The handle length affects required material strength. Everything connects.

This is the kind of physical product design I want to continue pursuing: thoughtful, functional, elegant solutions to real problems.
